# OSCP Study Plan <br />

## <span style="color:red">What is OSCP</span> 
OSCP stands for Offensive Security Certified Professional. It is a well-known and respected certification in the field of cybersecurity, specifically in the area of penetration testing and ethical hacking. OSCP is offered by Offensive Security, a leading provider of cybersecurity training and certifications.

<br />

The OSCP certification is highly regarded because it emphasizes practical skills and hands-on experience, making it a valuable credential for individuals aspiring to work as penetration testers, ethical hackers, or in other cybersecurity roles. It is often considered a milestone in a cybersecurity career and is recognized by many employers seeking professionals with practical cybersecurity skills.

<br />

## <span style="color:red">Why do I want OSCP</span> 


While some people argue that OSCP might not directly secure a job in cybersecurity, I personally view it as an entry point into the field.
<br />
Based on my research on platforms like Indeed and LinkedIn, it's evident that many companies still either require OSCP certification or consider it a valuable asset. The process of preparing for OSCP not only serves as motivation but also promises significant learning opportunities beyond aimless CTF solving.
<br />

Furthermore, OSCP can serve as a prerequisite for pursuing other advanced cybersecurity certifications. For instance, certifications like Offensive Security Certified Expert (OSCE) and Offensive Security Certified Web Expert (OSWE) mandate OSCP as a foundational requirement. Thus, achieving OSCP can serve as a stepping stone towards acquiring more advanced certifications.
<br />

## <span style="color:red">My plan</span> 

To pursue a career in cybersecurity as a penetration tester, I need to start preparing well before my college graduation.
<br />
My preparation will primarily focus on three key areas: **CTFs** (mainly HTB), **documentation**, and **branding**.


#### 1. CTF

I plan to tackle **two CTFs every week**, CONSISTENTLY. 

<br />My goal is to refrain from relying on others' writeups, and if I find it necessary, I'll limit my reference to specific sections rather than the entire solution. Once I've completed a challenge, I'll watch Ippsec's walkthrough videos to gain insights and learn from his problem-solving techniques.
<br />
I will be mainly solving boxes on [TJ_Null's list](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1dwSMIAPIam0PuRBkCiDI88pU3yzrqqHkDtBngUHNCw8/edit#gid=1839402159).


#### 2. Documentation

I will diligently create and upload well-structured WriteUps for two CTFs each week. These writeups will not be of the low-quality, hasty kind that rely on Metasploit. Instead, I'll ensure they are comprehensive, exploring various methods thoroughly. My aim is to produce professional-quality writeups that can serve as valuable additions to my portfolio in the future.

<br />
Writeups are going to be uploaded on my website on [HackTheBox Writeups](https://jadu101.github.io/writeups/htb)

#### 3. Branding


Building connections with others is crucial in the cybersecurity field. From my research, it seems like **LinkedIn** is the go-to platform for networking.
<br />
So, I'll set up and actively manage my LinkedIn profile to connect with fellow professionals. I want my profile to scream "I mean business," not just some newbie.
<br />

If there are any cybersecurity conferences happening nearby or online, I'll definitely try to attend. Staying in the loop with the latest cybersecurity buzz is on my radar, and I'll be tuning in to YouTube and checking out various cybersecurity news sites for that.
